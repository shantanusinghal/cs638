Editors’ Notes

Composer James Newton Howard confides that the Maleficent production team spent a considerable amount of time discussing the tone his score should take. “It’s a fantasy. A classic story. It’s quite a dark movie, yet a fairy tale,” he notes. “At first there was an emphasis on being darker. But my perspective all along was that it was a beautiful fairy tale.” Newton-Howard says that director Robert Stromberg requested a “classic” film score, and so he employed a massive studio orchestra and several choirs to produce a symphonic epic that often recalls Hollywood’s golden era and its European classical roots. “The ‘angry’ Maleficent was the most fun to write,” James says, “done in the Wagnerian tradition. A lot of the movie has that scope visually, and I tried to achieve the same with the music.” Yet he’s also quick to point out "the movie has terrific heart. You can write all the action music you want, but unless there’s a human connection to the story and characters it’s going to leave you feeling empty.”

James Newton Howard is Back, and “Maleficent” is Magnificent!
     
by AbinadiWitness

Wow! When I found out James Newton Howard was scoring Disney’s new movie, “Maleficent”, I figured it could go one of two ways with him…James Newton Howard has created some of my favorite film scores – but a lot of those have been a number of years ago, and much of his later work has been adequate but uninspiring on its own…I hoped this score would be able to stand with the likes of “Treasure Planet”, or “Dinosaur”, “Hidalgo”, or “Signs”…I hoped the generic action fluff music without much substance would be avoided which, unfortunately has come from him in recent years far too often in several movies that he’s scored…

Well, as I started listening through soundtrack samples online before the album became available for purchase, I began to quickly discover that the soundtrack to “Maleficent” would be something special – I remember remarking to some of my family members, “It sounds like James Newton Howard is back with his old, familiar magic.”

I am so pleased with the soundtrack – this is big, stirring, powerful, epic music on a vast orchestral scale. Now, that said, there are still plenty of intimate moments where things are close and soft, but when James Newton Howard needs to let loose the full force of the orchestra he does so, and the results are fantastic!

“Maleficent” stands comfortably with the best scores James Newton Howard has ever written, and I do not make that statement lightly or forget the high company Mr. Howard has set up for himself to keep over the years.

I can tell you this: If the movie is half as good as the music then it will be excellent, because the music is beautiful, stirring, thematically-driven, almost entirely orchestral (with some synth effects from time to time to augment that texture). One of the nice things about these tracks is that they are longer and allow legitimate thematic development; I remember listening to the first track and wondering if Mr. Howard was going to let me down – and then he grabbed hold of me and shook me with outstanding music that just kept coming and coming.

If you’ve ever enjoyed anything James Newton Howard has written then this deserves a place in your collection. If you enjoy well-crafted, exciting, stirring, impassioned orchestrally-driven music on a grand scale that soars and pounds in turn (yet that isn’t afraid to get soft and tender when it needs to), then you owe it to yourself to buy this album.

Hats off to James Newton Howard – this is the childhood magic I’ve been missing from you; thank you, for a tremendous musical treat through your “Maleficent” score!

Biography

Born: June 9, 1951 in Los Angeles, CA

Genre: Soundtrack

Years Active: '70s, '80s, '90s, '00s, '10s

Pianist, producer, and composer James Newton-Howard scored over 60 films beginning in the mid-'80s, including The Fugitive, The Prince of Tides, Pretty Woman, Glengarry Glen Ross, Batman Begins, Michael Clayton, and the Hunger Games series. Newton-Howard began taking classical piano lessons at the age of four, playing on a piano owned by his grandmother, who was the Pittsburgh Symphony's concertmaster and violinist during the 1930s and '40s.
